About Vintage Life

Vintage Life offers all the comfort of leading apparel e-commerce apps with a focus on secondhand. Finding and purchasing vintage clothing is as convenient as it gets without ever having to visit a physical store or use an outdated/rudimentary website.

The app is accessible through iOS devices or a web browser. Both versions have all the same functions (browse and buy apparel, save items for later, create an account for a quicker checkout). The web responsive version has an additional launch page that also advertises the app.

The Design Process

Project Planning

5 W's

Who

People who want to buy vintage apparel without going to a physical store.

What

A mobile app (and responsive website) that shows users the inventory and can be filtered in particular ways depending on their needs. Users can purchase without any goods and choose various payment methods.

When

This product will be used on commutes, in the evenings, during lunch breaks, and on weekends.

Where

Users can easily shop from anywhere they can connect to the internet, whether on a mobile device or on a desktop with the app (iOS only) or the responsive website.

Why

Users don't have the time or ability to visit physical stores.

Branding

Vintage Life is on a mission to make online secondhand apparel shopping convenient for all those who don’t have time to go to stores or are looking for that one elusive accessory that will complete their outfit.

Buying vintage is an environmentally friendly way to shop. The re-use of old clothing gives old garments another lease on life while minimizing the consumption of raw materials to produce new products.

No BS. Vintage Life may be online but it should feel accessible and laidback like your local vintage clothing store ! Still, what you see is what you get, just because the brand is casual does not mean Vitnage Life is not transparent. The overarching design emphasizes simplicity and confidence.

User Stories

The creation of user stories helped narrow down the scope of the project into manageable tasks. User needs and brief requirements helped identify the main features of the app.

View user stories

As a new customer, I want to access the inventory without having to register, so that I can make sure this store has what I’m looking for before having to create an account.

As a returning customer, I want to see an overview of my previous activity, so that I can view previous purchases and search history.

User Flow

With a clear understanding of the user in mind thanks to the user stories, user flows were the first step towards designing a solution. A rough idea of the required screens was subsequently developed leading to a user flow diagram representing all the steps a user will take to complete the tasks.

Defined Features

  • Homepage featuring special offers and recommendations
  • Search function with previous search history
  • Shopping bag preview page
  • Express or member checkout

Low to Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

I sketched the preliminary design ideas on paper before using Figma to create mid-fidelity wireframes that could be quickly prototyped for user testing.

User Testing

Usability testing was conducted remotely over Skype and Zoom with users ranging from the age of 26 to 33. Users were asked to accomplish 2 tasks:

“Imagine you have just installed Vintage Life, you want to browse the selection to see if there is anything you might want to buy before creating an account. Add some items to your wishlist before creating an account”.

“Imagine you were using the app earlier today and added several items to your cart that you are now ready to buy. Starting from the home screen, find your bag and check out.”

Positive Feedback

  • Easy navigation
  • Intuitive tab bar
  • No pain points when completing tasks

Constructive Feedback

  • The bag icon in the tab bar was not showing how many items were in the bag
  • Placeholders were missing on the checkout screen for date of birth
  • Item page had a lot of unused space above the picture
  • Inconsistent language (ex: both "sign in" and "log in" were used on different screens

All users succesfully completed the tasks, constructive feedback was given regarding some oversights that could hinder usability. Usability testing was fruitful in identifying minor design flaws that were quickly resolved. Feedback was otherwise very positive and no major setbacks were identified.

Colors

The brand color is a cobalt green. Green is universally linked with nature, representing growth and renewal which are all relevant associations for second hand shopping (i.e. more sustainable e-commerce). The cool cobalt saturation is both soothing and peaceful.

Black and white are the other two dominant colors. The secondary green is a lighter shade of the primary one keeping the palette limited to three colors (considering the secondary grey as an intermediary color). The result is a very clean and bright interface that is inviting in its simplicity as well as conveying transparency and trustworthiness.

Logo

The logo is the intersection of the first two letters of Vintage Life. The design is inspired by the Bauhaus approach to design where forms of expression are derived from function and dispense of the superfluous.

The flat and minimalist design evokes simplicity and a certain timelessness. The latter association is particularly coherent for vintage clothing that can find new life beyond the restrictive considerations of fast fashion.

The logotype was created with the Futura-Bold font. The “L” in “Life” has been slightly modified at the base to suggest a continuous line with the “V” of Vintage” that runs parallel to the right hand side of the logo.

The colors used are the same three primary ones used for the app.

The logo can be used on its own when space is limited. The logotype should always be used with the logo and on the right as shown.

Typography

The primary aim with the typography is to reinforce the functional and minimalist design.

Futura-Bold font makes for clean-looking headlines and text that is easy to read. Designed by Paul Renner and released in 1927, it was inspired by the concepts of Bauhaus.

Designed by the Dutch software developer Ralph Levien, Inconsolata has clean lines that make it highly readable.

Conclusion

What went well?

Building a cohesive brand from the start really helped to keep the design process all the way from low fidelity wireframes to the final mockups rather smooth. This helped each step iterate over the previous one with very little redesign or back and forth.

What were the challenges?

While waiting for additional feedback after my usability test I decided to start working on a web responsive version fo the app. Although not a part of the originally intended deliverables. Adding on more work when there was no need was an easily avoidable issue.

What could be improved?

For pragmatic reasons pictures were primarily sourced from free of use Pexel stock photos and were supplemented by some of my own. As a result the visual language lacks cohesion. Future iterations could have a more united visual style.
