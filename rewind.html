Mobile app Branding Concept iOS

Rewind is a concept for a Music Player app where users can listen to their favorite 80s music songs, create a profile and playlists and even sing along with scrolling lyrics. Additional functionalities include socials (Stream, liking, commenting and sharing) as well as an advanced search function.

responsibilities

UX Research, UX Design, UI Design

Show prototype

About Rewind

The concept for this app is a Music Player dedicated to the 1980s. This narrow focus would help distinguish the app from competitors by giving a more tailored experience while also infusing the layout with a unique fair inspired by design trends of the period.

The app was designed with iOS devices in mind. Besides listening to music the core functions of the app include creating a profile, searching for new artists as well as making playlists. Additionally, users can view lyrics when listening to a song, acess advanced search function with filters and use socials (comment on playlists, follow users).

The Design Process

User Flow

After completing a competitor analysis and a small sample of interviews, I started by creating a user flow detailing the different steps users will take to accomplish their goals. 3 main functionalities were defined for this project (create a profile , search for new artists and make a playlist). Preliminary research helped outline three additional functionalities (view lyrics when listening to a song, advanced search function and socials).

Low to Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Based on the desired functionalities and with the user flow in mind I started sketching wireframes. Sketches were then digitized into low fidelity wireframes in Figma followed by some usability testing with friends.

Although some ideas worked quite smoothly - the navigation bar at the bottom used a pattern consistent with other music apps - the advanced search function went through multiple iterations.

Mood Board

To establish the look and feel of the app I then created a mood board. The main inspiration was the “Memphis” design popular in the 80’s with its prominent use of geometric shapes and pastel shades.

Colors

Color scheme saw the most rework in order to strike a balance between nostalgic design and usability. After some setbacks, I restarted designing the color palette by creating the white “Memphis” background in Illustrator with geometric shapes and a colorful pastel palette. This way I was able to capture the look and feel of “Memphis” without overwhelming the user.

Subsequently, the main palette of the UI was designed to complement the background with a flat minimal aesthetic in mind. The dominant lilac purple complements well with the background and helps the layout standout while keeping it fun and retro. Black, off white and a sparse use of yellow and lavender accents help to keep a clean focus. Pure white (#FFFFFF) is used for text on purple backgrounds.

Logo

The logo is a figurative representation of a speaker inspired by a 1950s Josef Müller-Brockmann logo (Schule für Sprachtherapie in Graphic Design vol. 1, Jens Müller, 2021, p. 446) encircled by an arrow suggesting a rewind motion. Since the music app focuses on music from the 1980s the connection to the rewind action is easy to make, especially when thinking of cassettes which were one of the more prominent mediums to listen to music at the time. The logotype uses Poppins black capitalized with an inverted “R” to again suggest the idea of rewinding.

Typography

After multiple tries Poppins was finally chosen as the only typeface for the entire design. Geometric sans-serif typefaces are easily scalable. They were popular in the second half of the 20th century and tend to come across as less serious than serif ones.

Poppins stood out as good choice since besides being open source and free for Google fonts, it is also relatively new; helping it stay fresh and perhaps more modern than the more common Helvetica popular during the 80s.

Final Design

Conclusion

What went well?

The originality of the app’s look and feel helped give a lot of personality to the music app. Developing a strong visual identity early on helped articulate what would become the final design.

What were the challenges?

Since there were no equivalents in other music apps, designing the advanced search function took some time to figure out. Color scheme also took several iterations to succesfully translate the feel of Memphis design while remaining ergonomic.

What could be improved?

Designing a dark mode would be useful as it is standard (for good reason) across most music apps. Indeed, although the bright design of the app is striking it could be easier to use for extended periods of time with a darker color scheme.
