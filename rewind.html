Mobile app Branding Concept iOS

Rewind is a concept for a Music Player app where users can listen to their favorite 80s music songs, create a profile and playlists and even sing along with scrolling lyrics. Additional functionalities include socials (Stream, liking, commenting and sharing) as well as an advanced search function.

About Rewind

The concept for this app is a Music Player dedicated to the 1980s. This narrow focus would help distinguish the app from competitors by giving a more tailored experience while also infusing the layout with a unique fair inspired by design trends of the period.

The app was designed with iOS devices in mind. Besides listening to music the core functions of the app include creating a profile, searching for new artists as well as making playlists. Additionally, users can view lyrics when listening to a song, acess advanced search function with filters and use socials (comment on playlists, follow users).

The Design Process

User Flow

After completing a competitor analysis and a small sample of interviews, I started by creating a user flow detailing the different steps users will take to accomplish their goals. 3 main functionalities were defined for this project (create a profile , search for new artists and make a playlist). Preliminary research helped outline three additional functionalities (view lyrics when listening to a song, advanced search function and socials).

Low to Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Based on the desired functionalities and with the user flow in mind I started sketching wireframes. Sketches were then digitized into low fidelity wireframes in Figma followed by some usability testing with friends. Although some ideas worked quite smoothly - the navigation bar at the bottom used a pattern consistent with other music apps - the advanced search function went through multiple iterations.

User Testing

Usability testing was conducted remotely over Skype and Zoom with users ranging from the age of 26 to 33. Users were asked to accomplish 2 tasks:

“Imagine you have just installed Vintage Life, you want to browse the selection to see if there is anything you might want to buy before creating an account. Add some items to your wishlist before creating an account”.

“Imagine you were using the app earlier today and added several items to your cart that you are now ready to buy. Starting from the home screen, find your bag and check out.”

Positive Feedback

  • Easy navigation
  • Intuitive tab bar
  • No pain points when completing tasks

Constructive Feedback

  • The bag icon in the tab bar was not showing how many items were in the bag
  • Placeholders were missing on the checkout screen for date of birth
  • Item page had a lot of unused space above the picture
  • Inconsistent language (ex: both "sign in" and "log in" were used on different screens

All users succesfully completed the tasks, constructive feedback was given regarding some oversights that could hinder usability. Usability testing was fruitful in identifying minor design flaws that were quickly resolved. Feedback was otherwise very positive and no major setbacks were identified.

Colors

The brand color is a purple green. Green is universally linked with nature, representing growth and renewal (i.e. more sustainable e-commerce). The cool purple saturation is both soothing and peaceful.

Black and white are the other two dominant colors. The secondary green is a lighter shade of the primary one keeping the palette limited to three colors (considering grey as an intermediary). Resulting in a very clean and bright interface that is inviting.

Logo

The logo is the intersection of the first two letters of Vintage Life. The design is inspired by the Bauhaus approach to design where forms of expression are derived from function and dispense of the superfluous.

The logotype was created with the Futura-Bold font. The “L” in “Life” has been slightly modified at the base. The colors used are the same three primary ones used for the app. The flat and minimalist design evokes simplicity and a certain timelessness.

Typography

The primary aim with the typography is to reinforce the functional and minimalist design.

Futura-Bold font makes for clean-looking headlines and text that is easy to read. Designed by Paul Renner and released in 1927, it was inspired by the concepts of Bauhaus.

Designed by the Dutch software developer Ralph Levien, Inconsolata has clean lines that make it highly readable.

Conclusion

What went well?

Building a cohesive brand from the start really helped to keep the design process all the way from low fidelity wireframes to the final mockups rather smooth.

What were the challenges?

While waiting for additional feedback after my usability test I decided to start working on a web responsive version fo the app. Adding on more work when there was no need was an easily avoidable issue.

What could be improved?

For pragmatic reasons pictures were primarily sourced from free of use Pexel stock photos and were supplemented by some of my own. Future iterations could have a more united visual style.
